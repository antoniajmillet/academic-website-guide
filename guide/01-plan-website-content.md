# 1. Plan Your Website Content

[Return to the main guide](../README.md) · [Next: Plan your layout and design →](02-plan-layout-and-design.md)

Before thinking about colors, layout, or code, decide what you want people to learn about you and your work.

By the end of this chapter, you will know what sections your website needs and have the materials for each section gathered in one place.

## 1.1 Decide what visitors should understand

Imagine someone visiting your website for the first time. What are the most important things you want them to understand about you and your work?

For example:

- who you are;
- what you study or work on;
- what kinds of questions interest you;
- what kind of teacher or mentor you are;
- what you care about.

Complete this sentence:

> After visiting my website, I want someone to understand that I...

Your answer does not need to be polished. It will help you decide what information belongs on the website and what deserves the most attention.

## 1.2 Choose your website sections

Choose sections that support the ideas you identified above. A simple academic website might include:

```text
Home
About
Research or Projects
Publications
Teaching or Mentoring
CV
Contact
```

You do not need every section. Someone without publications could instead share posters, presentations, fieldwork, laboratory experience, a thesis, code, datasets, or ongoing projects.

## 1.3 Decide what belongs in each section

Make a short list of what you want each section to include.

| Section | Possible content |
| --- | --- |
| Home | Name, current position, research identity, portrait, and important links |
| About | Short biography and the interests that connect your work |
| Research or Projects | Major questions, projects, methods, and study systems |
| Publications | Publications, posters, presentations, or a Google Scholar link |
| Teaching or Mentoring | Teaching, mentoring, outreach, or service |
| CV | A link to your current CV |
| Contact | Email and professional profiles |

## 1.4 Gather your materials

Now that you know which sections you want, gather the information and files you may use on the website.

Create one folder on your computer called:

```text
website-materials
```

This is a planning folder. It keeps everything together before you begin building the website.

Organize it like this:

```text
website-materials/
├── writing/
├── images/
├── cv/
└── links.txt
```

You do not need to organize or rename every file perfectly yet. For now, focus on collecting what you may need.

### Gather your basic information

Create a file inside the `writing` folder called:

```text
basic-information.txt
```

Write down:

- your name exactly as you want it displayed;
- your current position;
- your university, organization, or laboratory;
- your professional email;
- your main research interests;
- any roles, fellowships, or affiliations you want to highlight.

### Collect your links

Add your important links to `links.txt`. These might include:

- Google Scholar;
- ORCID;
- GitHub;
- university or laboratory profile;
- publications;
- posters or presentations;
- datasets;
- project pages.

### Gather possible images

Place possible images in the `images` folder. These might include:

- a professional or field portrait;
- research organisms;
- field sites;
- laboratory or microscopy work;
- one image for each major project;
- clear figures from your work.

You do not need to resize or rename the images yet. You will prepare them when you begin building the website.

### Add your CV

Place the most recent PDF version of your CV in the `cv` folder.

## 1.5 Draft the writing for each section

Begin drafting the written content for the sections you selected. The writing does not need to be polished yet. Save each draft in the `writing` folder so you can easily find it later.

### Home

Write one or two sentences that quickly explain who you are and what you study or work on. Save this as:

```text
home-introduction.txt
```

### About

Draft two or three short paragraphs explaining:

- who you are now;
- how your interests developed;
- what connects your work.

Do not repeat everything already listed in your CV. Save this as:

```text
about.txt
```

### Research or Projects

For each project, answer:

1. What question are you asking?
2. Why is the question important or unresolved?
3. What system or method are you using?
4. What could the project help us understand?

Write so that someone outside your exact specialty can follow the main idea. Save these drafts as:

```text
project-descriptions.txt
```

### Teaching, Mentoring, Outreach, or Service

Describe the activities that are most important to your professional identity, such as:

- students you have mentored;
- courses you have taught or assisted with;
- outreach programs;
- educational resources;
- efforts to make research opportunities more accessible.

Save this as:

```text
teaching-mentoring.txt
```

Only create this section if it fits your work.

## 1.6 Review your planned content

Look back at the sentence you wrote at the beginning, then ask:

- Do my planned sections support that idea?
- Is any important information missing?
- Am I repeating information that can stay in my CV?
- Could someone outside my field understand the main ideas?

## Checkpoint

Before continuing, you should have:

- one sentence describing what visitors should understand;
- a list of website sections;
- a plan for what belongs in each section;
- your professional information;
- a file containing your important links;
- rough writing for each planned section;
- a folder of possible images;
- your current CV.
